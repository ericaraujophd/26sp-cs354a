## Class Policies

I want to make this course as enjoyable and rewarding as possible for you. To that end, I have a few policies that I want to share with you upfront. As some of you might know already, I am always open for feedback and suggestions on how to improve the course for the future.

---

### Regarding Technology in the Classroom

This course will require discipline and constant revision of the content, so classroom time is precious for learning core concepts. To maximize your focus and engagement during the lectures, **do not** use phones or laptops during class, unless you have a documented accommodation that requires you to do so. I know it might be hard at first, but I promise you that it will pay off in the long run.

I am also aware that some of you want to take notes on your laptops. Experience and research show that taking notes by hand helps you retain information better than typing them. If you really need to use a laptop for note taking, please sit in the back of the class to minimize distractions to your peers.

:::{admonition} Warning!
:class: warning
If you are filming, photographing, or recording audio in class, please ask for permission first. This is a violation of privacy and can make your classmates uncomfortable.
:::

### Regarding Late Work and Grading

Late submissions will be penalized **10% per working day** past the deadline. The last commit pushed to GitHub before the deadline is what counts as your submission — forgetting to push is not an acceptable excuse for lateness. Make it a habit to push early and often.

If you have extenuating circumstances, reach out to me **as early as possible** — the sooner you communicate, the more options we have.

## Academic Integrity

We take academic integrity very seriously. Cheating, plagiarism, and other forms of academic dishonesty will not be tolerated in this course. If you are caught cheating or plagiarizing, your situation will be dealt with in accordance with Calvin’s policies on Academic Honesty. Likewise, giving another person your work to submit as their own constitutes academic fraud and may result in a failing grade (F) for both parties.

Feel free to discuss ideas with us or with your classmates, but **don’t copy code** (i.e., plagiarize). Here are examples of what plagiarism looks like:

- You **copy** a SQL query or script from an online source, change some small things (e.g., column aliases, table names, or formatting), and submit it without attribution.
- Your roommate writes a query or script, which you incorporate into your own submission. You add comments that show you understand it, but never indicate the source.
- You find an online tutorial that walks you through the design of a schema or database and submit the resulting queries or scripts without attribution.
- You use a generative model (e.g., ChatGPT) to produce your code, review it, perhaps edit it a bit, and then submit it without attribution.

Consider these rules of thumb:

- If you find it efficient to use copy/paste or use a generative language model to create more than one or two lines of your query, script, or schema, you must document the original source.
- If the moment you figure out how to do something occurs while you are looking at a website or at the output of a generative language model, you should document that website.

Note that these rules of thumb apply to the code supplied in this course’s materials as well.

:::{admonition} Warning!
:class: warning
Our graders will be using a tool called the **Measure of Software Similarity (MOSS)** to check all submissions against all other submissions. If two submissions are found to be significantly similar, we will assume cheating has happened. Note that this software is effective even with SQL code — it can detect when two queries are essentially the same despite superficial differences such as aliasing, whitespace, clause reordering, or reformatted joins.
:::

### Shared Database Environments

In this course you will work with a shared PostgreSQL server. Each student has their own schema and data. **Accessing, querying, inspecting, or modifying another student's tables, schemas, or data — even without malicious intent — constitutes academic dishonesty** and will be treated accordingly. Stick to your own schema unless a collaborative assignment explicitly instructs otherwise.

---

### AI Tooling

The academic integrity policy above applies fully to AI-assisted SQL tools. This includes, but is not limited to, GitHub Copilot in a SQL editor, ChatGPT or other large language models used to generate queries, and any database-specific AI features (e.g., pgAdmin's AI assistant). You may use these tools to **understand** concepts, but any SQL you submit must be your own work. If an AI tool materially helped you arrive at a solution, document it as you would any other source.
